# Model Notes

This article uses conceptual justice-oriented transformation scoring models.

The scores are not objective moral truth. They are diagnostic tools for making distribution, participation, recognition, rights, accountability, ecological governance, intergenerational responsibility, and ethical risk visible.

## Core Concepts

- **Justice-oriented transformation**: estimates whether a resilience initiative distributes protection fairly, includes affected people, recognizes marginalized knowledge, protects rights, supports ecological repair, and remains accountable.
- **Ethical risk pressure**: estimates risk from maladaptation, harm-shifting, exclusion, coercion, displacement, and unequal burden.
- **Legitimacy-adjusted transformation**: combines technical resilience, justice-oriented transformation, public legitimacy, data transparency, and lower ethical risk.
- **Resilience justice gap**: compares legitimacy-adjusted transformation against ethical risk pressure.
- **Technical justice gap**: identifies cases where technical resilience is higher than justice-oriented transformation.
- **Harm-shifting**: risk transfer from more powerful groups or institutions to less powerful groups.
- **Recognition**: respect for histories, cultures, identities, knowledge systems, dignity, and affected communities.

## Interpretation

A resilience initiative can be technically strong and ethically weak.

The model is most useful for climate adaptation, disaster recovery, energy transition, infrastructure relocation, urban resilience, ecological restoration, public-service reform, public accountability reviews, and resilience governance audits.
