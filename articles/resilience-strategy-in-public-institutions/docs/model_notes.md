# Model Notes

This article uses conceptual public institutional resilience scoring models.

The scores are not objective certifications of public-sector readiness. They are diagnostic tools for making institutional strengths and weaknesses visible across foresight, continuity, administrative capacity, coordination, finance, procurement, digital fallback, legitimacy, justice, and learning.

## Core Concepts

- **Institutional resilience capacity**: estimates whether a public institution can anticipate disruption, preserve core functions, coordinate, finance resilience, procure under stress, maintain fallback systems, and learn.
- **Institutional fragility pressure**: estimates risk from fragmentation, underinvestment, staffing fragility, digital dependency, procurement vulnerability, and accountability gaps.
- **Legitimacy-adjusted resilience**: adjusts institutional capacity by public legitimacy, service equity, and accountability.
- **Institutional resilience gap**: compares legitimacy-adjusted resilience against fragility pressure.
- **Continuity of operations**: preservation of essential public functions under disruption.
- **Risk-informed finance**: budgeting, investment, maintenance, and contingency funding aligned with risk.
- **Digital fallback**: manual, offline, human, accessible, or alternate service pathways when digital systems fail.
- **Learning and adaptation**: ability to revise institutional practice after exercises, shocks, audits, and community feedback.

## Interpretation

A public institution can appear functional in normal time while remaining brittle under stress.

The model is most useful for public-sector resilience audits, institutional stress testing, public-service continuity planning, procurement resilience, local government readiness, and public-administration reform.
