# Model Notes

This article uses conceptual poverty-fragility and development resilience scoring models.

The scores are not objective certifications of development fragility. They are diagnostic tools for making risk exposure, multidimensional poverty, institutional weakness, livelihood precarity, service deficits, conflict pressure, climate stress, displacement pressure, social protection, household buffers, adaptive capacity, service continuity, institutional trust, and community voice visible.

## Core Concepts

- **Development fragility**: estimates the degree to which development gains are vulnerable to reversal under stress.
- **Resilience sufficiency**: estimates whether households, communities, and institutions have enough buffers to absorb disruption.
- **Poverty-fragility gap**: compares resilience sufficiency against development fragility.
- **Shock amplification**: describes how one disruption triggers cascading losses across income, health, education, food security, debt, displacement, and trust.
- **Development scarring**: describes long-lasting harm after shocks, including lost schooling, debt, asset loss, chronic illness, trauma, and institutional distrust.
- **Resilient development**: poverty reduction designed to withstand climate, conflict, economic, health, and service-disruption shocks.

## Interpretation

A place can have visible development gains while remaining fragile if multidimensional poverty, livelihood precarity, weak services, conflict pressure, climate stress, and institutional weakness exceed social protection and adaptive capacity.

The model is most useful for poverty-reduction strategy, social-protection planning, climate adaptation, fragile-context programming, infrastructure prioritization, service-continuity planning, and public legitimacy analysis.
