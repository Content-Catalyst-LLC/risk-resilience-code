# Model Notes

This article uses conceptual regenerative resilience and living-systems repair scoring models.

The scores are not objective ecological truth. They are diagnostic tools for making regenerative capacity, degradation pressure, restoration integrity, governance accountability, justice, and repair gaps visible.

## Core Concepts

- **Regenerative capacity**: estimates whether ecosystem integrity, biodiversity, soil health, water function, connectivity, stewardship, governance, justice, and monitoring support living-systems repair.
- **Degradation pressure**: estimates chronic stress from ecological degradation, fragmentation, extraction, pollution, climate stress, and maladaptation.
- **Living-systems repair gap**: compares regenerative capacity against degradation pressure.
- **Restoration integrity**: estimates whether restoration is producing functional recovery, biodiversity recovery, hydrological repair, community stewardship, and justice outcomes.
- **Ecological memory**: represents seed banks, soil organisms, surviving roots, species pools, hydrological patterns, local knowledge, and recovery pathways.
- **Maladaptation risk**: represents the risk that interventions shift harm, simplify ecosystems, displace communities, or create superficial green outcomes.

## Interpretation

A landscape can look greener without becoming more regenerative. Regenerative resilience requires functional ecological recovery, justice, stewardship, monitoring, and accountable governance.

The model is most useful for restoration prioritization, watershed planning, biodiversity governance, regenerative agriculture review, nature-based resilience, climate adaptation, urban ecological infrastructure, and community stewardship evaluation.
