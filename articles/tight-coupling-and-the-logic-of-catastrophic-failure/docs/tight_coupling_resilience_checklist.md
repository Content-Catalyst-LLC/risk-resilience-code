# Tight Coupling and Catastrophic-Failure Checklist

Use this checklist when reviewing critical infrastructure, hospitals, digital systems, supply chains, utilities, public services, emergency response systems, or other time-sensitive systems.

## Diagnosing Tight Coupling

- Which processes must happen in strict sequence?
- How much time exists between disturbance and downstream consequence?
- Can the system pause safely?
- Are substitutions available if one component fails?
- Are critical dependencies mapped?
- Are there hidden dependencies on power, data, staffing, communications, chemicals, logistics, or finance?

## Complexity and Interaction

- Are system interactions fully understood?
- Could multiple alarms or failures appear at once?
- Could automation hide the state of the system from operators?
- Could backup systems depend on the same failed system as the primary system?
- Are near misses reviewed as evidence of hidden coupling?

## Resilience Room

- Are buffers maintained and tested?
- Are modular boundaries or firebreaks present?
- Are credible redundant pathways available?
- Do local actors have authority to pause, reroute, or improvise?
- Are manual fallback procedures practiced?
- Can the system degrade gracefully rather than fail suddenly?

## Justice and Public Protection

- Who lacks private buffers if the system fails?
- Which households, workers, patients, communities, or regions absorb coupling risk?
- Are public protections in place for those with the least slack?
- Are emergency communications accessible, multilingual, and trusted?
- Does the system rely on workers as hidden shock absorbers?

## Governance

- Are cross-sector dependency maps current?
- Are stress tests conducted across sectors?
- Are critical nodes protected by public accountability?
- Are procedures designed to support judgment rather than replace it?
- Are catastrophic-failure scenarios used to redesign systems before crisis?
