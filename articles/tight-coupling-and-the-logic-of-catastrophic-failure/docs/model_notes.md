# Model Notes

This article uses conceptual tight-coupling and catastrophic-failure scoring models.

The scores are not objective predictions of accidents or disasters. They are diagnostic tools for making coupling strength, time compression, sequence rigidity, limited substitution, interactive complexity, hidden dependency, critical-node importance, buffering, modularity, redundancy, adaptive authority, and fallback capacity visible.

## Core Concepts

- **Tight-coupling pressure**: the degree to which failures can move rapidly through rigid, time-sensitive dependencies.
- **Resilience room**: the available space for diagnosis, intervention, substitution, containment, and repair.
- **Catastrophic-failure risk**: the risk that local disruption escalates rapidly into systemic failure.
- **Containment margin**: the difference between resilience room and tight-coupling pressure.
- **Time compression**: how little time exists between disturbance and downstream consequence.
- **Interactive complexity**: unexpected interactions among components, technologies, procedures, and institutions.
- **Adaptive authority**: the ability of responsible actors to pause, reroute, improvise, or act locally.

## Interpretation

A system can be highly efficient and still catastrophically fragile if it compresses time, removes buffers, eliminates substitutes, and ties many functions to critical nodes.

The model is most useful for critical-infrastructure review, cyber resilience, hospital preparedness, utility planning, supply-chain design, interdependency mapping, public-sector stress testing, and resilience governance.
