# Model Notes

This article uses conceptual nonlinear shock-propagation and containment-capacity scoring models.

The scores are not objective predictions of disaster outcomes. They are diagnostic tools for making shock intensity, threshold proximity, network centrality, coupling strength, feedback amplification, hidden stress, exposure inequality, buffering capacity, modularity, redundancy, adaptive response, and governance quality visible.

## Core Concepts

- **Propagation pressure**: the force pushing a disturbance beyond its point of origin.
- **Containment capacity**: the system's ability to absorb, isolate, reroute, and adapt before disruption cascades.
- **Nonlinear propagation risk**: the risk that a disturbance produces disproportionate, cross-system consequences.
- **Propagation resilience margin**: the difference between containment capacity and propagation pressure.
- **Threshold proximity**: how close a system is to a boundary beyond which behavior changes abruptly.
- **Network centrality**: whether the disturbed node is highly connected or systemically important.
- **Feedback amplification**: reinforcing dynamics that intensify the original shock.

## Interpretation

A small shock can have large consequences when it hits a system near a threshold, strikes a central node, or activates feedback loops. A large shock can remain contained when buffers, modularity, redundancy, adaptive response, and governance quality are strong.

The model is most useful for infrastructure planning, climate-risk analysis, public-health preparedness, cyber resilience, ecological monitoring, emergency management, and systemic-risk governance.
